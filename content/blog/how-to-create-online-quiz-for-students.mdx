## Step 2: Write Questions That Actually Test Knowledge

This is where most teachers mess up.

Bad question:
> "What is photosynthesis?"

Good question:
> "A plant needs sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make food. What is this process called?"

See the difference? The second one actually tests if they understood the concept, not just memorized a word.

**Rule I follow:** For every question, imagine a student who paid attention but didn't memorize the textbook. Can they answer it?

If yes → Good question.
If no → Rewrite it.

## Common Mistakes I Made (So You Don't Have To)

**Mistake 1: Too many questions**

My first online quiz had 25 questions. Students took 45 minutes. Several just closed the tab halfway.

**Fix:** Keep it under 12 questions. Short and focused beats long and exhausting.

**Mistake 2: Unclear instructions**

I once wrote "Answer all questions" and assumed that was enough. Students kept asking "Do I need to write full sentences?"

**Fix:** Add a short instruction box: "Choose the best answer for each multiple-choice question. You have 15 minutes."

**Mistake 3: Not testing the quiz myself**

I shared a quiz without previewing it. Two questions had the wrong correct answer marked. Students were confused and frustrated.

**Fix:** Always take your own quiz before publishing. Takes 2 minutes. Saves hours of complaints.

**Mistake 4: Ignoring mobile users**

One of my students took the quiz on his phone. The text was tiny, buttons were hard to click. He got distracted and gave up.

**Fix:** Check your quiz on a phone screen before sharing.

## Quick FAQ (Real Questions Teachers Asked Me)

**How many questions should a quiz have?**
Start with 8-12. Adjust based on how long students take. If most finish early, add more next time.

**Can students cheat?**
Some will try. Randomizing questions and limiting time helps. But honestly, cheating happens on paper tests too. Focus on teaching, not policing.

**Do I need to buy a paid plan?**
No. Free plans on most tools are enough for one teacher. Upgrade only if you need advanced reports or more than 100 students.

**What if a student has slow internet?**
Most tools save answers as they go. Tell students to take the quiz when internet is stable. Offer a paper alternative if needed.

**How do I help struggling students?**
Use the analytics. See which questions they missed. Review those topics individually or in a small group.
